Theravāda Vinayapiṭaka

Monks’ rules and their analysis

Monks’ Expulsion 2: Theft

Origin story

First sub-story

At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Rājagaha on Mount Vulture’s Peak. At that time a number of monks who were friends had made grass huts on the slope of Mount Isigili and had entered the rains there. Among them was Venerable Dhaniya, the potter’s son. After three months, when the rains residence was over, the monks demolished their grass huts, put away the grass and wood, and departed to wander around the country. But Venerable Dhaniya also spent the winter and the summer there. Then, while Venerable Dhaniya was in the village collecting alms, some women gathering grass and firewood demolished the grass hut and took away the grass and sticks. A second time Venerable Dhaniya collected grass and sticks and made a grass hut, and again the hut was demolished in the same way. The same thing happened a third time.

Venerable Dhaniya thought, “Three times this has happened. But I am well-trained and experienced in my own craft, the potter’s craft. Perhaps I should knead mud myself and make a hut consisting of nothing but clay?” Then Venerable Dhaniya did just that. And he collected grass, wood, and cow-dung, and he baked his hut. It was a beautiful, lovely, and charming little hut, and it was red just like a scarlet rain-mite. And (when hit) it sounded just like the sound of a bell.

Just then the Master, descending from Mount Vulture’s Peak with a number of monks, saw that hut. He then addressed the monks: “Monks, what is this beautiful, lovely, and charming thing, which is red like a scarlet rain-mite?” And the monks informed the Master. The Buddha, the Master, was critical, saying:

“It’s not suitable, monks, for that foolish man, it’s not becoming, it’s not proper, it’s not worthy of an ascetic, it’s not allowable, it’s not to be done. How could he make a hut out of nothing but clay? Certainly that foolish man can have no consideration, compassion, and mercy for living beings. Go, monks, and demolish this hut. Do not let future generations take up the destruction of living beings. And, monks, a hut consisting of nothing but clay should not be made. If one does, there is an offence of bad conduct.”

“Yes, bhante,” the monks replied, and they went to that hut and demolished it.

Venerable Dhaniya said to them: “Why are you demolishing my hut?”

“The Master has asked us to.”

“Demolish it, if the Lord of the Dhamma has said so.”

Then Venerable Dhaniya thought: “Three times, while I was in the village collecting alms, some women gathering grass and firewood demolished my hut and took away the grass and sticks. And now my hut made of nothing but clay has been demolished at the Master’s request. But the overseer of the wood-yard is a friend of mine. Perhaps I should ask him for wood and make a hut out of that?”

And Venerable Dhaniya went to the overseer of the wood-yard and told him what had happened, adding, “Give me some wood, I want to make a wooden hut.”

“There is no such wood, bhante, that I could give you. This wood is held by the king, and it’s meant for the repair of the town and put aside in case of emergency. Only if the king gives it may you take it.”

“It has been given by the king.”

The overseer of the wood-yard thought: “These ascetics, the sons of the Sakyan, are followers of dhamma, of just conduct, committed to the spiritual life, speakers of truth, virtuous, of good conduct. Even the king has faith in them. He would not say something is given if it were not.” Then he said to Venerable Dhaniya: “You may take it, bhante.” Then Venerable Dhaniya had that wood broken up into pieces, removed it by means of carts, and made a wooden hut.

Soon afterwards the brahmin Vassakāra, the chief minister of Magadha, while inspecting the works in Rājagaha, went to the overseer of the wood-yard and said to him: “What’s going on? Where’s the wood held by the king that’s meant for the repair of the town and put aside in case of emergency?”

“Sir, that wood was given by the king to Venerable Dhaniya.”

The brahmin Vassakāra was upset and thought: “How could the king give away the wood, which is meant for the repair of the town and put aside in case of emergency, to Dhaniya, the potter’s son?”

The brahmin Vassakāra then went to King Seniya Bimbisāra of Magadha and said: “Is it true, sire, that the wood held by the king, meant for the repair of the town and put aside in case of emergency, was given by the king to Dhaniya, the potter’s son?”

“Who said that?”

“The overseer of the wood-yard, sire.”

“Well then, brahmin, send for the overseer of the wood-yard.” And Vassakāra had the overseer of the wood-yard bound and taken by force.

Venerable Dhaniya saw the overseer of the wood-yard being bound and taken by force, and he said to him: “Why is this happening to you?”

“Because of the business with that wood, bhante.”

“Go, and I too will come.”

“Please come before I am done for, bhante.”

Venerable Dhaniya then went to the dwelling of King Bimbisāra and sat down on the appointed seat. King Bimbisāra approached Venerable Dhaniya, paid homage to him, and sat down to one side. King Bimbisāra then said: “Is it true, bhante, that the wood held by the king, meant for the repair of the town and put aside in case of emergency, has been given by me to the Venerable?”

“Yes, great king.”

“We kings are very busy—having given, we may not remember. Please remind me, bhante.”

“Do you remember, great king, when you were first anointed, speaking these words: ‘Let the ascetics and brahmins enjoy gifts of grass, sticks, and water’?”

“I remember, bhante. There are ascetics and brahmins who have a sense of shame, are scrupulous, and desirous of training. They have a sense of conscience even in regard to small matters. What was uttered by me was meant for these, and it concerned what is ownerless in the wilderness. Yet you, bhante, imagine you can take wood not given to you by means of this trick? But how could I presume to beat, imprison, or banish an ascetic or a brahmin living in my kingdom? Go, you are free because of your status, but do not do such a thing again.”

But people grumbled and complained: “These ascetics, the sons of the Sakyan, are shameless, immoral, and liars. They claim to be followers of dhamma, of just conduct, committed to the spiritual life, speakers of truth, virtuous, of good conduct. But they don’t have the qualities of an ascetic or a brahmin; they have lost their way. They even deceive the king; what then other people?”

Monks heard the criticism of those people. Those monks who had few desires and a sense of shame, who were contented, scrupulous, and desirous of training, complained and criticised Venerable Dhaniya: “How can he take wood belonging to the king that has not been given to him?”

They informed the Master. The Master then convened the Sangha and questioned Venerable Dhaniya: “Is it true, Dhaniya, that you have taken wood belonging to the king that was not given to you?”

“It’s true, Master.”

The Buddha, the Master, rebuked him: “Foolish man, it’s not suitable, it is not becoming, it’s not proper, it’s not worthy of an ascetic, it’s not allowable, it’s not to be done. How could you take wood belonging to the king that had not been given to you? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it, nor increase the confidence of those who have it, but it will hinder confidence in those without it and it will cause some with confidence to change their minds.”

At that time a certain former chief justice who had gone forth among the monks was sitting near the Master. The Master said to him: “For what amount of theft does King Bimbisāra beat, imprison, or banish a thief?”

“For a pāda, Master, for the worth of a pāda, or for more than a pāda.” At that time in Rājagaha a pāda was worth five māsakas.

The Master, having rebuked Venerable Dhaniya in many ways for being difficult to support … “… And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Preliminary ruling

“If a monk, intending to steal, takes what has not been given to him—the sort of theft for which kings, having caught a thief, would beat, imprison, or banish him, saying, ‘You are a robber, you are foolish, you have gone astray, you are a thief’—he too is expelled and not in communion.”

In this way the Master laid down this training rule for the monks.

Second sub-story

At one time some monks belonging to the group of six went to the dyers’ spread (of dyed cloth), took the dyers’ goods to the monastery and shared it out. Other monks said: “You have great merit, since you have obtained so many robes.”

“How is there merit for us? Just now we went to the dyers’ spread (of dyed cloth) and took the dyers’ goods.”

“Hasn’t a training rule been laid down by the Master? Why then do you take the dyers’ goods?”

“It is true that a training rule has been laid down by the Master, but it concerns the village, not the wilderness.”

“But that’s just the same. It’s not suitable, it’s not becoming, it’s not proper, it’s not worthy of an ascetic, it’s not allowable, it’s not to be done. How can you take the dyers’s goods? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it, nor increase the confidence of those who have it, but it will hinder confidence in those without it and it will cause some with confidence to change their minds.”

Having rebuked those monks in various ways, they informed the Master. The Master convened the Sangha and questioned those monks: “Is it true, monks, that you went to the dyers’ spread (of dyed cloth) and took the dyers’ goods?”

“It’s true, Master.”

The Buddha, the Master, rebuked them: “It’s not suitable, foolish men, it is not becoming, it’s not proper, it’s not worthy of an ascetic, it’s not allowable, it’s not to be done. How could you go to the dyers’ spread (of dyed cloth) and take the dyers’ goods? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … to change their minds.” Then the Master, after rebuking those monks belonging to the group of six in various ways, spoke in dispraise of being difficult to support … but he spoke in praise … of putting forth energy. Having given a Dhamma talk on what is right and seemly, he addressed the monks … “… And so, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

“If a monk, intending to steal, takes from a village or from the wilderness what has not been given to him—the sort of theft for which kings, having caught a thief, would beat, imprison, or banish him, saying, ‘You are a robber, you are foolish, you have gone astray, you are a thief’—he too is expelled and not in communion.”

Definitions

A: whoever … Monk: … The monk ordained by a complete Sangha through a procedure of one motion and three announcements, which is unchallengeable and fit to stand—this sort of monk is meant in this case.

A village: a village of one hut, a village of two huts, a village of three huts, a village of four huts, an inhabited village, an uninhabited village, a fenced-in village, a village which is not fenced in, a disorganised village, and even a caravan that is camping for more than four months is called a village. The precincts of a village: of a fenced-in village, the outward stone-throw of a man of average height standing at the threshold (of the village gate); of a village which is not fenced in, the outward stone-throw of a man of average height standing within the precincts of a house.

The wilderness: leaving aside the village and the precincts of the village, what remains is called “the wilderness.”

What has not been given: what has not been given, nor let go of, nor thrown away; what is guarded, protected, regarded as “mine”; what belongs to others—this is called “what has not been given.”

Intending to steal: the thought of stealing, the thought of taking.

If (he) takes: if (he) takes, if (he) carries off, if (he) takes away, if (he) interrupts the mode of movement, if (he) removes from its base, if (he) does not fulfil an obligation.

The sort of theft: a pāda, the worth of a pāda, or more than a pāda.

Kings: kings of the earth, local kings, governors of islands, governors of border areas, judges, chief ministers, or whoever metes out physical punishment—these are called “kings”.

A thief: one who, intending to steal, takes anything having the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas that has not been given—he is called “a thief”.

Would beat: they would beat with the hand, the foot, a whip, a cane, a rod, or by maiming.

Would imprison: they would imprison by constriction with a rope, by constriction with fetters, by constriction with chains, by constriction to a house, by constriction to a town, by constriction to a village, by constriction to a small town, by being guarded.

Would banish: they would banish from a village, from a small town, from a town, from a province, or from a rural district.

You are a robber, you are foolish, you have gone astray, you are a thief: this is censure.

He too: this is said with reference to the preceding offence entailing expulsion.

Is expelled: just as a fallen, withered leaf cannot become green again, so a monk who, intending to steal, takes a pāda, the worth of a pāda, or more than a pāda, which had not been given to him, is not an ascetic, not a son of the Sakyan—therefore it is said “he is expelled.”

Not in communion:
Communion: a common official action, the same recital, the same training—this is called “communion.”
He does not take part in this—therefore it is called “not in communion.”

Permutations

Permutations part 1

Summary

Being in the earth, being on firm ground, being in the air, being above ground, being in water, being in a boat, being in a vehicle, carried as a burden, being in a park, being in a monastic dwelling, being in a field, being on a plot of land, being in a village, being in the wilderness, water, tooth-wood, forest tree, that which is carried, that which is deposited, customs station, a living being, footless, two-footed, four-footed, many-footed, a spy, a keeper of entrusted property, a mutually agreed theft, an arranged action, the making of a sign.

Exposition

Being in the earth: the goods are put down into the earth, buried, covered. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will take the goods in the earth,” and he seeks for a companion, or he seeks for a hoe or a basket, or he just goes (to the goods), he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he breaks a piece of wood or a slender tree growing there, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he digs up the soil or removes it or lifts it up, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches the container, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he makes it move from its base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he puts his own vessel into (the container) and touches something worth five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he makes it enter his own vessel or takes it with his fist, there is an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he touches goods held together by a string, or a bracelet, a necklace, an ornamental girdle, a robe, or a turban, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he grasps it at the top and pulls it, he commits a serious offence. If, while stroking it, he lifts it, he commits a serious offence. If he removes the goods even as much as a hair’s breadth over the rim of the container, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal with a single act, he drinks ghee, oil, honey, or molasses to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he destroys it, throws it away, burns it, or renders it useless, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

Being on firm ground: the goods are put down on firm ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods on firm ground,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Being in the air: the goods are in the air. A peacock, a partridge, or a quail. A robe, a turban, or gold: being cut, it falls. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the air,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he cuts off their course of movement, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Being above ground: the goods are above ground. On a bed, on a bench, on a bamboo pole for hanging robes, on a cord for hanging robes, on a peg in the wall, on an elephant tusk peg, in a tree, or even if just fastened to a bowl stand. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods that are above ground,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Being in the water: the goods are deposited in the water. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the water,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. He either dives into the water or emerges from it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches the goods, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he touches either a blue, red, or white lotus growing there, or the sprout of a lotus, or a fish, or a turtle to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A boat: that by mean of which one crosses. Being in a boat: the goods are deposited in a boat. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods deposited in the boat,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the boat,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he loosens the moorings, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, after loosening the moorings, he touches it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he makes it move up or down, or across the water, even for as much as a hair’s breadth, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A vehicle: a litter, a two-wheeled carriage, a cart, a chariot. Being in a vehicle: the goods are deposited in a vehicle. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods deposited in the vehicle,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the vehicle,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves it from its base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A burden: a burden carried on the head, a burden carried on the back, a burden carried on the hip, one hanging down. If, intending to steal, he touches the burden on the head, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he lowers it to the back, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If, intending to steal, he touches the burden on the back, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he lowers it to the hip, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If, intending to steal, he touches the burden on the hip, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he causes it to quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he takes it with a hand, there is an offence entailing expulsion. If, intending to steal a burden in the hand, he deposits it on the ground, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If, intending to steal, he takes it from the ground, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A park: a park with flowers, an orchard. Being in a park: the goods are deposited in the park in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the park,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he touches a root growing there, or a piece of bark, a leaf, a flower, or a fruit to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he claims the park, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he evokes doubt in the owner as to his ownership, he commits a serious offence. If the owner thinks, “I won’t get it back,” and he gives up the effort of reclaiming it, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law and defeats the owner, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law but is defeated, he commits a serious offence.

Being in a monastic dwelling: the goods are deposited in a monastic dwelling in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the monastic dwelling,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he claims the monastic dwelling, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he evokes doubt in the owner as to his ownership, he commits a serious offence. If the owner thinks, “I won’t get it back,” and he gives up the effort of reclaiming it, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law and defeats the owner, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law but is defeated, he commits a serious offence.

A field: where grain or vegetables grow. Being in a field: the goods are deposited in a field in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods deposited in the field,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he touches the grain or the vegetables which grow there to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he claims the field, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he evokes doubt in the owner as to his ownership, he commits a serious offence. If the owner thinks, “I won’t get it back,” and he gives up the effort of reclaiming it, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law and defeats the owner, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law but is defeated, he commits a serious offence.

If he shifts a post, a cord, a fence, or a boundary, he commits an offence of bad conduct. When one action of the shifting remains, he commits a serious offence. When the last action of the shifting is completed, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A plot of land: the plot of land of a park or a monastery, the plot of land of a monastic dwelling. Being on a plot of land: the goods are deposited on a plot of land in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods on the plot of land,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he claims the plot of land, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he evokes doubt in the owner as to his ownership, he commits a serious offence. If the owner thinks, “I won’t get it back,” and he gives up the effort of reclaiming it, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law and defeats the owner, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law but is defeated, he commits a serious offence.

If he shifts a post, a cord, a fence, or a boundary, he commits an offence of bad conduct. When one action of the shifting remains, he commits a serious offence. When the last action of the shifting is completed, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Being in a village: the goods are deposited in a village in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the village,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

The wilderness: any wilderness which is owned by people. Being in the wilderness: the goods are deposited in the wilderness in four places: in the earth, on the firm ground, in the air, above the ground. If, intending to steal, he thinks, “I will steal the goods in the wilderness,” and he either searches for a companion or just goes himself, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he touches a piece of wood belonging there, or a creeper or grass, to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves it from its base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Water: in a vessel, in a pond, or in a reservoir. If, intending to steal, he touches it, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves it from its base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, intending to steal, he puts his own vessel into (the container holding the water), and he touches water to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he puts it into his own bowl, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he breaks the embankment, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, after breaking the embankment, he empties water to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he empties water to the value of more than one māsaka but less than five māsakas, he commits a serious offence. If he empties water to the value of a māsaka or less than a māsaka, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

Tooth-wood: either cut or uncut. If, intending to steal, he touches what has a value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes it quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves it from its base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Forest tree: whatever tree is owned by people. If, intending to steal, he fells it, for each blow he commits an offence of bad conduct. When one blow remains (before the tree is felled), he commits a serious offence. When the last blow is completed, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Goods being carried: the goods of another are being carried. If, intending to steal, he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “Together with the carrier I will carry off the goods,” and he makes the carrier move one foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes him move the second foot, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “I will seize the fallen goods,” and he makes them fall, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, intending to steal, he touches the fallen goods to the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Deposit: goods deposited with oneself. If one is told, “Give me my goods,” and one says, “I’m not getting them,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he evokes doubt in the mind of the owner (as to whether he will get them back), he commits a serious offence. If the owner thinks, “He won’t give them to me,” and he gives up the effort of getting them back, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he resorts to the law and defeats the owner, he commits an offence entailing expulsion. If he resorts to the law but is defeated, he commits a serious offence.

Customs station: it is established by a king in a mountain-pass, at a ford in a river, or at the gate of a village, so that tax shall be received from a person passing through. If, intending to steal, and having entered there, he touches goods that have a (tax) value to the king of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he goes beyond the customs station with one foot, he commits a serious offence. If he goes beyond the customs station with his second foot, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If, standing within the customs station, he makes the goods fall outside the customs station, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he avoids the customs station, he commits an offence of bad conduct.

Creature: a human being. If, intending to steal, he touches him, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes him quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves him from his base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “I will take him away on foot,” and he makes him move the first foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes him move the second foot, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Footless: snakes and fish. If, intending to steal, he touches what has the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Two-footed: humans and birds. If, intending to steal, he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “I will take them away on foot,” and he makes them move the first foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes them move the second foot, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Four-footed: elephants, horses, camels, cattle, asses, domesticated animals. If, intending to steal, he touches them, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “I will take them away on foot,” and he makes them move the first foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes them move the second foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes them move the third foot, he commits a serious offence. If he makes them move the fourth foot, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

Many-footed: scorpions, centipedes, caterpillars. If, intending to steal, he touches what has the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

If he thinks, “I will take them away on foot,” and he makes them move, he commits a serious offence for each leg that moves. When the last leg moves, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A spy: having spied out goods. If he describes them and says, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he takes those goods, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both.

A keeper of entrusted property: guarding goods that have been brought to him. If, intending to steal, he touches what has the value of five māsakas or more than five māsakas, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he makes them quiver, he commits a serious offence. If he moves them from their base, he commits an offence entailing expulsion.

A mutually agreed theft: many have agreed together. If only one steals the goods, there is an offence entailing expulsion for all of them.

An arranged action: one makes an arrangement for before the meal or for after the meal, for the night or for the day. If he says, “Take those goods according to this agreement,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he takes those goods according to that agreement, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both. If he takes those goods before or after the time of the agreement, there is no offence for the instigator, but an offence entailing expulsion for the thief.

The making of a sign: he makes a sign. If he says, “When I wink, raise my eyebrow, or nod, at that sign steal the goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, at that sign, he steals the goods, he commits an offence entailing expulsion for both. If he steals the goods before or after the sign, there is no offence for the instigator, but an offence entailing expulsion for the thief.

Permutations part 2

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he steals them, thinking they are the ones he has been asked to steal, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” but he steals something else, thinking they are the ones he has been asked to steal, there is no offence for the instigator, but there is an offence entailing expulsion for the thief.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” and he steals them, thinking they are something else than what he has been asked to steal, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” but he steals something else than what he has been asked to steal, thinking it is something else, there is no offence for the instigator, but there is an offence entailing expulsion for the thief.

If a monk tells a monk, “Tell such and such to tell such and such to steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. In informing the next person, there is commits an offence of bad conduct. If the (potential) thief agrees, there is a serious offence for the instigator. If he steals those goods, there is an offence entailing expulsion for all of them.

If a monk tells a monk, “Tell such and such to tell such and such to steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he tells someone else than the one he was told to tell, he commits an offence of bad conduct. If the (potential) thief agrees, there is an offence of bad conduct. If he steals those goods, there is no offence for the instigator, but there is an offence entailing expulsion for the conveyor of the message and the thief.

If a monk tells a monk, “steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. Having gone, he returns, saying, “I’m not able to steal those goods,” and if he tells him again, “When you are able, then steal those goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If he steals the goods, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, having told him, he regrets it, but does not say to him, “Don’t steal,” and he then steals those goods, there is an offence entailing expulsion for both.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, having told him, he regrets it and says, “Don’t steal,” but the other says, “I have been told by you to do so,” and he then steals those goods, there is no offence for the instigator, but an offence entailing expulsion for the thief.

If a monk tells a monk, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits an offence of bad conduct. If, having told him, he regrets it and says to him, “Don’t steal,” and he says, “Very well,” and desists, there is no offence for either.

Non-offences

There is no offence: if he perceives it as his own; if he takes it on trust; if he borrows it; if it is the possession of a ghost or an animal; if he perceives it as discarded; if he is insane; if he is deranged; if he is overwhelmed by pain; if he is the first offender.

At one time, as a certain monk was crossing a river, a robe that had escaped from the hands of dyers, stuck to his foot. The monk took hold of it, thinking, “I will give it to the owners.” The owners reprimanded that monk, saying, “You are no longer an ascetic.” He became anxious … “There is no offence for one who does not intend to steal.”

At one time, as a certain monk was crossing a river, a robe that had escaped from the hands of dyers stuck to his foot. The monk took hold of it, intending to steal, thinking, “Before the owners see it.” The owners reprimanded the monk, saying, “You are no longer a ascetic.” He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time a certain monk, seeing a pot of ghee, ate it little by little. He became anxious … “There is no offence entailing expulsion, but there is an offence of bad conduct.”

At one time a number of monks, having made an arrangement, went away, thinking, “We will steal these goods.” One of them stole the goods. The others said, “We are not expelled; the one who took it is expelled.” They informed the Master. He said, “You have all fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time a number of monks, having made an arrangement, stole some goods and shared them out. When it was being shared out, none of them received a share that amounted to five māsakas. They said, “We are not expelled.” They informed the Master. He said, “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time, when Sāvatthī was short of food, a certain monk, intending to steal, took a handful of rice belonging to a shopkeeper. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time, when Sāvatthī was short of food, a certain monk, intending to steal, took a handful of kidney-beans … a handful of beans … a handful of sesamum belonging to a shopkeeper. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time some thieves in the Dark Wood near Sāvatthī, having killed a cow, ate some of the flesh, put the remainder aside, and went away. Some monks, perceiving it as discarded, had it offered and ate it. The thieves reprimanded those monks, saying, “You are no longer ascetics.” They became anxious … “There is no offence for one who perceives it as discarded.”

At one time some thieves in the Dark Wood near Sāvatthī, having killed a pig … “There is no offence for one who perceives it as discarded.”

At one time a certain monk went to a meadow and, intending to steal, took some cut grass worth five māsakas. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time a certain monk went to a meadow and, intending to steal, cut grass worth five māsakas and took it away. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time some visiting monks divided up the mangoes belonging to the Sangha and ate them. The resident monks reprimanded those monks, saying, “You are no longer ascetics.” They became anxious. They informed the Master.

“What was your intention, monks?”

“We thought they were meant for eating, Master.”

“There is no offence for one who thinks it is meant for eating.”

At one time some visiting monks divided up the rose-apples belonging to the Sangha … the bread-fruit belonging to the Sangha … the jack-fruit … the palm fruits … the sugar-cane … the timbarūsaka fruits belonging to the Sangha and ate them. The resident monks … “There is no offence for one who thinks it is meant for eating.”

At one time the keepers of a mango-grove gave a mango to some monks. The monks, thinking, “They have the authority to guard, but not to give,” had scruples and did not accept it. They informed the Master. He said, “There is no offence when it is a gift from a guardian.”

At one time the keepers of a rose-apple grove … a timbarūsaka grove gave timbarūsaka fruit to some monks. The monks, thinking, “They have the authority to guard, but not to give” … “There is no offence when it is a gift from a guardian.”

At one time a certain monk, having borrowed a piece of wood belonging to the Sangha, used it to support the wall of his own dwelling. The monks reprimanded that monk, saying, “You are no longer an ascetic.” He became anxious and informed the Master. He said, “What was your intention, monk?”

“I was borrowing it, Master.”

“There is no offence for one who is borrowing.”

At one time a certain monk, intending to steal, took water belonging to the Sangha … clay belonging to the Sangha … intending to steal, he took a pile of tiṇa-grass belonging to the Sangha. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time a certain monk, intending to steal, set fire to a pile of tiṇa-grass belonging to the Sangha. He became anxious … “There is no offence entailing expulsion, but there is an offence of bad conduct.”

At one time a certain monk, intending to steal, took a bed belonging to the Sangha. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time a certain monk, intending to steal, took a bench belonging to the Sangha … took a cushion … a pillow … a door … a window … intending to steal, he took a rafter belonging to the Sangha. He became anxious … “You have fallen into an offence entailing expulsion.”

At one time the furnishings and requisites for a dwelling belonging to a certain lay follower were used elsewhere by some monks. That lay-follower complained and criticised them: “How can those Venerables use elsewhere requisites belonging somewhere else?” They informed the Master. “One should not use elsewhere requisites belonging somewhere else. If one does, there is an offence of bad conduct.”

At one time some monks, having scruples about taking requisites to the uposatha hall or to a meeting place, sat down on the ground. Their limbs and robes were covered with dust. They informed the Master. “I allow you to borrow.”

At one time at Campā a nun who was a pupil of the nun Thullanandā went to the family who supported the Thullanandā and said, “The Venerable wants to drink rice-gruel containing the three pungent ingredients.” When it was ready, she took it away and ate it herself. When the Thullananadā found out about this, she reprimanded her, saying, “You are no longer an ascetic.” She became anxious. She then informed the nuns, who in turn informed the monks, who then informed the Master. “There is no offence entailing expulsion, but in a deliberate lie there is an offence entailing confession.”

At one time in Rājagaha a nun who was a pupil of the nun Thullanandā went to the family who supported the Thullanandā and said, “The Venerable wants to eat a honey-ball.” When it was ready, she took it away and ate it herself. When the Thullanandā found out about this … “There is no offence entailing expulsion, but in a deliberate lie there is an offence entailing confession.”

At one time in Vesāli a householder who was a supporter of Venerable Ajjuka had two children living with him, a son and a nephew. That householder said to Venerable Ajjuka, “Bhante, would you please show this location to the one of these two boys who has faith and confidence.”

At that time the householder’s nephew had faith and confidence, and so Venerable Ajjuka showed that location to him. He then established a household with that wealth and made a gift.

Then the householder’s son said to Venerable Ānanda: “Bhante Ānanda, who is the father’s heir, the son or the nephew?”

“The son is the father’s heir.”

“Bhante, this Venerable Ajjuka has shown our wealth to our cohabitant.”

“Venerable Ajjuka is no longer an ascetic.”

Then Venerable Ajjuka said to Venerable Ānanda: “Venerable Ānanda, please investigate.”

At that time Venerable Upāli was siding with Venerable Ajjuka, and he said to Venerable Ānanda: “Ānanda, when one is asked by the owner to show a location to so-and-so, and one does so, what offence has one committed?”

“Bhante, one has not committed any offence, not even one of bad conduct.”

“Venerable Ajjuka was asked by the owner to show a location to so-and-so, and he did so. There is no offence for Venerable Ajjuka.”

Now at that time in Benares the family that supported Venerable Pilindavaccha was harassed by criminals, and two children were kidnapped. Venerable Pilindavaccha brought the children back by his psychic power and placed them in a large house.

People saw those children and said: “This is the majesty of the psychic power of Venerable Pilindavaccha,” and they placed confidence in Venerable Pilindavaccha.

The monks complained and criticised him: “How can this Venerable Pilindavaccha bring back children who have been kidnapped by criminals?” They informed the Master. He said: “There is no offence in the area of psychic powers for one who possesses them.”

At one time the two monks Paṇḍaka and Kapila were friends. One lived in a village and one at Kosambī. Then, while the first monk was going from the village to Kosambī, as he was crossing a river, a lump of fat that had escaped from the hands of pork-butchers stuck to his foot. He took hold of it, thinking, “I will give it to the owners.” The owners reprimanded him, saying, “You are no longer an ascetic.”

Then a woman cowherd who had seen him crossing said, “Come, bhante, have sexual intercourse.” Thinking, “I am no longer an ascetic,” he had sexual intercourse with her.

When he arrived in Kosambī, he informed the monks and they informed the Master. He said, “There is no offence entailing expulsion for stealing, but there is an offence entailing expulsion for having sexual intercourse.”

At that time at Sāgalā a monk who was the disciple of Venerable Daḷhika, being tormented by discontent, took a turban from a shopkeeper. He then said to Venerable Daḷhika, “Bhante, I am no longer an ascetic; I will disrobe.”

“But what have you done?” He told him. Having had the turban brought, Venerable Daḷhika had it valued, and it was not worth five māsakas. “There is no offence entailing expulsion,” and he gave a talk on the Dhamma. That monk was delighted.

The second offence entailing expulsion is finished.
